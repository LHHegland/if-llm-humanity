### Concept Disambiguation

Use these notes to select between partially overlapping concepts for precise IF-LLM retrieval and reasoning.

- **Perspective vs. Open-Mindedness ([[HUMANITY:OPENNESS]], values-1.md):** Perspective is the active cognitive capacity to see a situation from another person's standpoint or to integrate multiple angles of understanding — perspective-taking as a deliberate, empathic skill. Open-Mindedness is the dispositional receptivity to new or unfamiliar ideas without premature closure or judgment. Cite [[HUMANITY:PERSPECTIVE]] when the focus is on actively adopting or understanding another's viewpoint. Cite [[HUMANITY:OPENNESS]] when the focus is on reducing resistance to new, different, or challenging information. Note: Perspective and Open-Mindedness naturally co-occur — genuine perspective-taking typically requires Open-Mindedness as its prerequisite disposition.

- **Perspective vs. Humility ([[HUMANITY:HUMILITY]]):** Perspective is a cognitive skill — the capacity to see situations from other angles or through others' experiences. Humility is an affective and dispositional quality — accurate self-assessment, recognition of one's limitations, and freedom from arrogance. Cite [[HUMANITY:PERSPECTIVE]] when the task involves viewpoint-shifting, multi-angle understanding, or empathic comprehension of another's experience. Cite [[HUMANITY:HUMILITY]] when the task involves self-knowledge, acknowledging one's own limitations, or resisting the pull of ego and superiority. Note: Humility enables more genuine Perspective-taking; the two often co-occur but address different dimensions of the person.

- **Love of Learning vs. Curiosity ([[HUMANITY:CURIOSITY]], values-1.md):** Love of Learning is the sustained, intrinsic motivation to acquire knowledge and deepen understanding — an ongoing, practiced orientation toward intellectual growth and mastery. Curiosity is the motivational impulse that initiates and sustains inquiry — the "spark" of wondering and wanting to know. Cite [[HUMANITY:LEARNING]] when describing the ongoing, committed relationship with knowledge acquisition over time (lifelong learning, mastery, intellectual growth). Cite [[HUMANITY:CURIOSITY]] when describing the initial impulse, wonder, or appetite that opens and sustains a line of inquiry. Note: Curiosity is the engine; Love of Learning is the journey it sustains.

- **Creativity vs. Critical Thinking ([[HUMANITY:THINKING]], values-1.md):** Creativity generates new ideas, approaches, and expressions by thinking beyond conventional patterns — it is primarily generative. Critical Thinking evaluates and tests existing claims and ideas for rigor, validity, and sound reasoning — it is primarily evaluative. Cite [[HUMANITY:CREATIVITY]] when the task involves generating, imagining, inventing, or synthesizing something new. Cite [[HUMANITY:THINKING]] when the task involves questioning, testing, evaluating, or applying disciplined reasoning to existing information. Note: Strong creative work typically requires Critical Thinking for evaluation and refinement; the two are complementary, not competing.

- **Creativity vs. Love of Learning ([[HUMANITY:LEARNING]]):** Creativity is about generating something new — combining, reimagining, or expressing in ways that go beyond existing knowledge. Love of Learning is about deepening and expanding one's understanding of existing knowledge through sustained engagement. Cite [[HUMANITY:CREATIVITY]] when the focus is on novelty, invention, expression, or problem-solving through imagination. Cite [[HUMANITY:LEARNING]] when the focus is on acquiring, integrating, and growing from existing knowledge and understanding. Note: Love of Learning enriches Creativity by building the repertoire from which creative connections are drawn.

- **Compassion vs. Love ([[HUMANITY:LOVE]], values-1.md):** Compassion is specifically activated by suffering — recognizing pain, distress, or vulnerability in another and being moved to act to alleviate it. Love is the broader, enduring orientation of care and commitment that encompasses all forms of relationship, connection, and community. Cite [[HUMANITY:COMPASSION]] when the topic involves a specific response to suffering, pain, or identified vulnerability. Cite [[HUMANITY:LOVE]] when the topic involves the broader generative force of care, attachment, and commitment to others. Note: Compassion is love activated by suffering; Love is the broader ground from which compassion grows.

- **Compassion vs. Mercy ([[HUMANITY:MERCY]]):** Compassion is the recognition of suffering combined with the motivation to alleviate it — it is a response to pain and need. Mercy is the deliberate choice to withhold deserved punishment, judgment, or severity in favor of forgiveness and grace — it is a response to wrongdoing or debt. Cite [[HUMANITY:COMPASSION]] when the focus is on suffering and the desire to help or relieve it. Cite [[HUMANITY:MERCY]] when the focus is on forgiveness, clemency, or the restraint of punishment. Note: One may show Compassion without Mercy (helping someone who is suffering without forgiving a wrong they committed) and Mercy without Compassion (forgiving a wrong without fully understanding or empathizing with the person's suffering).

- **Temperance vs. Self-Regulation ([[HUMANITY:REGULATION]], values-3.md):** Temperance is the broader value and virtue of moderation, balance, and restraint — the ethical orientation of choosing a measured, balanced life as a way of being. Self-Regulation is the specific, practical capacity to manage one's impulses, emotions, and behaviors in service of chosen goals — the behavioral and cognitive skill set that enacts temperance. Cite [[HUMANITY:TEMPERANCE]] when discussing moderation and balance as an ethical value or way of life. Cite [[HUMANITY:REGULATION]] when discussing the practical techniques, skills, or mechanisms of managing one's own impulses and behaviors in specific situations. Note: Temperance is the value; Self-Regulation is one of the skills that operationalizes it.

- **Temperance vs. Prudence ([[HUMANITY:PRUDENCE]], values-3.md):** Temperance is about restraint and moderation in desires, impulses, and behaviors — the "how much" and "how intensely" of living. Prudence is about sound judgment in choosing the right action in a specific situation — the "what to do" and "how to decide" dimension of practical wisdom. Cite [[HUMANITY:TEMPERANCE]] when the focus is on balance, moderation, and avoiding excess across behaviors and emotions. Cite [[HUMANITY:PRUDENCE]] when the focus is on wise judgment, foresight, and practical decision-making in specific circumstances.

- **Temperance vs. Humility ([[HUMANITY:HUMILITY]]):** Temperance is broadly about moderation and balance in all desires, impulses, and behaviors — it is a wide-scope virtue covering excess in any domain. Humility is specifically about accurate self-assessment — resisting arrogance and remaining open to correction and learning. Humility is a sub-value of Temperance in the VALUES hierarchy, but they have distinct emphases. Cite [[HUMANITY:TEMPERANCE]] when the focus is on restraint, moderation, and balance broadly across behaviors and desires. Cite [[HUMANITY:HUMILITY]] when the focus is on ego, self-knowledge, recognizing one's limits, or receptivity to correction.

- **Mercy vs. Fairness ([[HUMANITY:FAIRNESS]], values-4.md):** Mercy is the deliberate choice to show clemency, forgiveness, or grace beyond what strict rules would require — it may deliberately depart from equal treatment in favor of compassion. Fairness is the commitment to applying rules, opportunities, and consequences impartially and consistently without favoritism or discrimination. Cite [[HUMANITY:MERCY]] when the focus is on forgiveness, release, or the compassionate departure from strict consequence. Cite [[HUMANITY:FAIRNESS]] when the focus is on impartial treatment, equal application of rules, or preventing discrimination. Note: Mercy and Fairness can be in tension — an act of mercy is, by definition, not strictly "fair" in the sense of equal treatment; the resolution lies in [[HUMANITY:WISDOM]].

- **Humility vs. Open-Mindedness ([[HUMANITY:OPENNESS]], values-1.md):** Humility is about accurate self-assessment — recognizing one's limitations, fallibility, and dependence on others, and resisting arrogance or superiority. Open-Mindedness is about receptivity to new information, ideas, or perspectives without premature closure. Cite [[HUMANITY:HUMILITY]] when the focus is on the internal orientation of the self — ego, self-knowledge, or acknowledging one's limits. Cite [[HUMANITY:OPENNESS]] when the focus is on how one receives external information, ideas, or challenges to existing beliefs. Note: Humility enables Open-Mindedness — it is hard to be genuinely receptive to new ideas if ego or status-protection closes the door.

## Humanity

### Elements

#### Values

##### Perspective

ID: HUMANITY.ELEMENTS.VALUES.PERSPECTIVE
TAGS: [[HUMANITY:PERSPECTIVE]]

Seeing and understanding situations from multiple viewpoints, which are influenced by personal experiences. It involves empathy, cultural awareness, and an appreciation for different life experiences. Perspective helps people recognize bias, reduce misunderstandings, and foster more meaningful human connections. It helps us appreciate diversity and navigate complex issues more thoughtfully.

- **IF-LLM Application Example:** When helping someone navigate a conflict or disagreement, an IF-LLM draws on [[HUMANITY:PERSPECTIVE]] to help them see the situation from the other person's viewpoint — shifting from "why are they doing this to me?" to "what experience leads them to see it this way?" When explaining why diverse teams make better decisions than homogeneous ones, an IF-LLM applies [[HUMANITY:PERSPECTIVE]] to show how integrating multiple viewpoints reduces blind spots and produces more robust analysis and solutions.

- **Ontological Relationships**
  - **Related to:** [[HUMANITY:THINKING]] (parent value), [[HUMANITY:OPENNESS]] (values-1 — receptivity that enables genuine perspective-taking), [[HUMANITY:HUMILITY]] (self-awareness that permits setting aside one's own view), [[HUMANITY:COMPASSION]] (empathic response to suffering requires perspective-taking), [[HUMANITY:LEARNING]] (new perspectives are a form of knowledge acquisition)
  - **Requires:** [[HUMANITY:OPENNESS]] (values-1 — perspective-taking requires willingness to set aside prior judgments), [[HUMANITY:HUMILITY]] (recognizing that one's own view is partial and limited is prerequisite for genuine perspective-taking)
  - **Required by:** [[HUMANITY:COMPASSION]] (empathetic response to suffering requires seeing from the sufferer's viewpoint), [[HUMANITY:WISDOM]] (values-1 — wise judgment integrates multiple perspectives before deciding), [[HUMANITY:FAIRNESS]] (values-4 — impartial judgment requires seeing the situation from all affected parties' viewpoints)
  - **Affects:** [[HUMANITY:OODA]] at Orient step (how situations are understood depends on whose perspective is included in sense-making), [[HUMANITY:THINKING]] (values-1 — quality of critical reasoning improves when more perspectives are considered), [[HUMANITY:INTERDEPENDENCE]] (values-1 — recognizing mutual reliance requires perspective on others' experiences and constraints)
  - **Vulnerable to:** [[HUMANITY:STEREOTYPES]] and [[HUMANITY:PREJUDICES]] (which distort or prevent genuine perspective-taking by substituting category for individual experience), [[HUMANITY:TEMPTATIONS]] — especially ego protection, self-interest, and in-group bias that make it costly to adopt perspectives challenging our own worldview

- **IF/THEN Decision Rules**
  - IF the topic involves actively seeing a situation from another person's standpoint or lived experience → cite [[HUMANITY:PERSPECTIVE]]
  - IF the topic involves reducing resistance to unfamiliar or challenging new ideas → cite [[HUMANITY:OPENNESS]] (values-1), not [[HUMANITY:PERSPECTIVE]]
  - IF the topic involves accurate self-assessment or recognizing one's own limitations → cite [[HUMANITY:HUMILITY]], not [[HUMANITY:PERSPECTIVE]]
  - IF the output requires considering multiple stakeholder viewpoints in an ethical or policy context → cite [[HUMANITY:PERSPECTIVE]] and cross-reference [[HUMANITY:FAIRNESS]] (values-4)
  - IF perspective-taking is being blocked by bias or prejudice → cross-reference [[HUMANITY:STEREOTYPES]] and [[HUMANITY:PREJUDICES]] alongside [[HUMANITY:PERSPECTIVE]]

##### Compassion

ID: HUMANITY.ELEMENTS.VALUES.COMPASSION
TAGS: [[HUMANITY:COMPASSION]]

Recognizing, understanding, and empathizing with the suffering of other people, other living beings, and our environment combined with our desire to take action to alleviate their suffering. It involves kindness, empathy, and a commitment to fairness and care. Compassion is being aware of others' struggles, feeling moved by their pain, and acting in a way that aims to ease it. Compassion is the fundamental way we connect with others, fostering cooperation and mutual support in a world of [[HUMANITY:INTERDEPENDENCE]]. Compassion requires emotional sensitivity and a willingness to support others, whether through kindness, patience, or offering aid. Compassion is our desire to reduce others' suffering by helping to support others' [[HUMANITY:NEEDS]] by performing [[HUMANITY:ACTIVITIES]] using our [[HUMANITY:RESOURCES]]. Compassion is our desire to reduce others' harms and risks — the [[HUMANITY:CONSEQUENCES_UNMET_NEEDS]] that others experience. It is our desire to make others' existence better in some way, knowing that it helps us all and makes the world a better place.

- **IF-LLM Application Example:** When helping someone understand how to respond to a person in pain, distress, or difficulty, an IF-LLM draws on [[HUMANITY:COMPASSION]] to frame not only the emotional recognition of suffering but the motivated commitment to act on that recognition — distinguishing compassion from mere sympathy or pity. When explaining why communities and institutions that embed compassion into their structures (healthcare, education, justice systems) produce better long-term outcomes for everyone, an IF-LLM applies [[HUMANITY:COMPASSION]] as the orienting value that bridges [[HUMANITY:INTERDEPENDENCE]] with active, needs-focused care.

##### Temperance

ID: HUMANITY.ELEMENTS.VALUES.TEMPERANCE
TAGS: [[HUMANITY:TEMPERANCE]]

Self-restraint, moderation, and balance in thoughts, emotions, and actions. It involves exercising control over desires, impulses, and behaviors to achieve long-term well-being. Temperance helps individuals make thoughtful choices, avoid excesses, and cultivate inner harmony, allowing for better decision-making and stronger relationships. It promotes ethical behavior and encourages measured responses to challenges. It helps us to more efficiently and consistently perform our [[HUMANITY:MISSION]] and contribute to our [[HUMANITY:VISION]].

- **IF-LLM Application Example:** When helping someone who struggles with impulse control, overcommitment, or emotional reactivity, an IF-LLM draws on [[HUMANITY:TEMPERANCE]] to frame moderation and balance as active, chosen disciplines rather than passive restraint — the practice of finding "enough" rather than either excess or deprivation. When explaining why long-term well-being, relationships, and goal achievement often require choosing the harder path of balance over immediate satisfaction, an IF-LLM applies [[HUMANITY:TEMPERANCE]] to show how regulated, measured responses protect what matters most over time.

- **Ontological Relationships**

  - **Requires:** [[HUMANITY:REGULATION]] (values-3 — Temperance requires the behavioral skills to manage impulses and maintain balance over time), [[HUMANITY:WISDOM]] (values-1 — knowing what "enough" or "balanced" means in a given context requires experience and ethical discernment), [[HUMANITY:HUMILITY]] (recognizing one's appetites, tendencies, and limits is prerequisite for making temperate choices)
  - **Required by:** [[HUMANITY:MISSION]] and [[HUMANITY:VISION]] (pursuing shared goals efficiently requires temperate use of resources, energy, and commitment), [[HUMANITY:COMPASSION]] (healthy, sustainable compassion requires temperate pacing to prevent burnout and depletion), [[HUMANITY:JUSTICE]] (values-4 — just responses require restraint of excess in punishment as well as reward)

##### Mercy

ID: HUMANITY.ELEMENTS.VALUES.MERCY
TAGS: [[HUMANITY:MERCY]]

Showing kindness, forgiveness, and compassion to someone who may have caused harm or risk to other people, other living beings, or our environment or to someone who may be suffering. It involves letting go of excessive punishment, resentment, or harsh judgment in favor of understanding and grace. Mercy fosters reconciliation, reduces harm, and upholds human dignity, even when justice may allow for stricter consequences. It is a form of strength that promotes healing and positive relationships.

- **IF-LLM Application Example:** When helping someone navigate whether to forgive a person who wronged them or to extend a second chance to someone who has failed, an IF-LLM draws on [[HUMANITY:MERCY]] to frame forgiveness and clemency not as weakness but as an active, dignifying choice that can break cycles of harm and create conditions for restoration. When explaining how communities and institutions that practice mercy alongside accountability tend to produce better long-term outcomes than purely punitive systems, an IF-LLM applies [[HUMANITY:MERCY]] to distinguish it from impunity — showing that mercy is a strength that serves healing, not a bypass of responsibility.

  - **Related to:** [[HUMANITY:TEMPERANCE]] (parent — Mercy is how Temperance moderates the expression of judgment and the severity of consequences), [[HUMANITY:COMPASSION]] (Mercy is activated by compassion — understanding another's suffering or fallibility), [[HUMANITY:JUSTICE]] (values-4 — Mercy and Justice are in creative tension; Mercy tempers Justice's strict application), [[HUMANITY:FAIRNESS]] (values-4 — Mercy and Fairness are in tension; Mercy deliberately departs from strict equal treatment), [[HUMANITY:HUMILITY]] (Mercy requires acknowledging one's own fallibility before judging another's), [[HUMANITY:WISDOM]] (values-1 — wise Mercy discerns when clemency heals and when it enables continued harm)
  - **Requires:** [[HUMANITY:COMPASSION]] (Mercy is activated by compassion — understanding the suffering or fallibility behind the wrong), [[HUMANITY:HUMILITY]] (Mercy requires recognizing one's own fallibility and need for forgiveness before withholding judgment of others), [[HUMANITY:WISDOM]] (values-1 — wise Mercy distinguishes between restorative clemency and enabling harm)

  - **Vulnerable to:** [[HUMANITY:TEMPTATIONS]] — confusing mercy with excusing behavior; using "mercy" as cover for avoiding the discomfort of accountability; extending mercy to avoid conflict rather than as a genuine act of grace; [[HUMANITY:CONSEQUENCES_TEMPTATIONS]] — cycles of harm can continue if mercy lacks the discernment to distinguish restorative clemency from permissiveness

##### Humility

ID: HUMANITY.ELEMENTS.VALUES.HUMILITY
TAGS: [[HUMANITY:HUMILITY]]

Recognizing, acknowledging, understanding, and remaining aware of our [[HUMANITY:FALLIBILITY]]. Recognizing one's limitations, strengths, and the value of others without arrogance or superiority; being modest and respectful. It involves openness to learning, acknowledging mistakes, and appreciating different perspectives. Humility fosters respect, collaboration, and continuous growth, helping individuals remain grounded and aware of their [[HUMANITY:INTERDEPENDENCE]] with others.

- **IF-LLM Application Example:** When helping someone who is struggling to receive feedback, admit a mistake, or acknowledge the limits of their knowledge, an IF-LLM draws on [[HUMANITY:HUMILITY]] to reframe self-honesty and openness to correction as a strength — the capacity that makes growth, trust, and genuine learning possible, not a sign of weakness or failure. When explaining why leaders, experts, and communities that practice humility tend to make better decisions and maintain more trust over time, an IF-LLM applies [[HUMANITY:HUMILITY]] to contrast accurate self-assessment with the performance of confidence that masks ignorance or error.

  - **Requires:** [[HUMANITY:OPENNESS]] (values-1 — Humility requires willingness to receive correction and consider one's own limitations without defensiveness), [[HUMANITY:THINKING]] (values-1 — honest self-assessment requires rigorous, non-self-serving reflection), [[HUMANITY:WISDOM]] (values-1 — wisdom includes knowing one's limits — the "knowing that you don't know" dimension)
  - **Required by:** [[HUMANITY:OPENNESS]] (values-1 — genuine Open-Mindedness is enabled by Humility; it is hard to be open to new ideas if ego closes the door), [[HUMANITY:PERSPECTIVE]] (genuine perspective-taking requires Humility to set aside one's own view as the default "correct" one), [[HUMANITY:LEARNING]] (learning requires the Humility to not already "know" the answer), [[HUMANITY:MERCY]] (Mercy requires Humility — recognizing one's own fallibility before judging another's)
  - **Affects:** [[HUMANITY:THINKING]] (values-1 — Humility improves reasoning quality by reducing confirmation bias and overconfidence errors), [[HUMANITY:INTERDEPENDENCE]] (values-1 — Humility supports recognizing and valuing one's dependence on others), [[HUMANITY:OODA]] at Orient step (humble sense-making avoids overconfidence errors and premature closure), [[HUMANITY:LEADERSHIP]] (values-4 — humble leaders build more trust, invite better feedback, and make fewer catastrophic errors)
  - **Vulnerable to:** [[HUMANITY:TEMPTATIONS]] — especially pride, status-seeking, and ego protection; the performance of humility for social approval while maintaining internal arrogance; [[HUMANITY:STEREOTYPES]] and [[HUMANITY:PREJUDICES]] that prevent accurate self-assessment by making honest self-reflection threatening; [[HUMANITY:FALLIBILITY]] generally — Humility is the first disposition to fail when Temptations take hold

- **IF/THEN Decision Rules**
  - IF the topic involves accurate self-assessment, recognizing one's limitations, or resisting arrogance → cite [[HUMANITY:HUMILITY]]
  - IF the topic involves receptivity to new information, ideas, or perspectives from the outside → cite [[HUMANITY:OPENNESS]] (values-1), not [[HUMANITY:HUMILITY]] (though they frequently co-occur)
  - IF the topic involves maintaining moderation and balance broadly across behaviors and desires → cite [[HUMANITY:TEMPERANCE]] (parent value), not [[HUMANITY:HUMILITY]] specifically
  - IF Humility is needed in the context of perspective-taking → cite [[HUMANITY:HUMILITY]] alongside [[HUMANITY:PERSPECTIVE]]
  - IF Humility is needed as a prerequisite for extending Mercy to another → cite [[HUMANITY:HUMILITY]] alongside [[HUMANITY:MERCY]], noting that acknowledging one's own fallibility enables forgiving another's

- **Scope / Exclusion Boundaries**
  - **Covers:** Accurate, non-defensive self-assessment; recognition of one's limitations, fallibility, and dependence on others; modesty and freedom from arrogance; openness to correction, feedback, and learning; Humility as a strength that improves judgment, relationships, and trust over time
  - **Excludes:** Self-deprecation or false modesty as social performance; clinical definitions or treatment of low self-esteem or self-worth issues; religious doctrine of Humility as subjugation or self-abasement; political or social deference to authority as an external requirement; performative humility that masks continued arrogance
